About this site

One thing that I, like many others, struggle with is comparing myself to those that are experts in their craft. I see their work and think "They are so talented", completely ignoring the hundreds and hundreds of hours of work that they've put into their work. This site serves as a reference to myself and others by showing my journey into photography. Of course, I want to show off my work that I am most proud of, but I also want to provide resources to make it easier for others to follow.

My Journey into Astro (and Rocket) Photography

It all started with a move to Orlando. Being less than an hour away from the space coast in the midst of the second space-race, I became very interested in watching the launches. I could go outside and see a bright (and I mean bright) flame rise from the horizon, turn away from me, and eventually disappear as it reached space. I wanted to see more, so I got a pair of Celestron 7x50 binoculars. Now I could actually see the rocket on top of the flame. Once again, I wanted more.

I started driving out to see the rockets from the coast. I ended up getting an annual pass for the Kennedy Space Center. This has been worth its money times over. I was able to see SLS on the pad from mere feet away. Then, I watched a Falcon 9 launch from a few miles away. The next Falcon 9 launch returned back to the launch site and landed at Cape Canaveral, rattling the bleachers with a sonic boom. I was able to enjoy these moments, but my friends and family back home were too far. It was at this point that I wanted to start photographing the launches.

SLS taken from just 1600ft away.

It started off quite humble. I had recorded with my phone, but that doesn't quite capture the feeling. I have a GoPro Session that I thought could work. Using an upside down tripod mount, I attached the GoPro to my binoculars but that still wasn't enough. I wanted a real camera to capture these moments. After scouring Ebay and every other used marketplace that you can think of, I came across a Canon 550D on Facebook. It came with 2 lenses, one of which was a 55mm-250mm zoom lens, which I knew I'd need for the distances I was dealing with. I had my camera and I was ready to go.

My first attempt at moon photography.

There were no launches scheduled around this time, so I'd turn my attention to another target, the Moon. I knew enough about manual photography to be dangerous, so away I went to take some pictures of the strawberry moon. They were a shaky disaster. I didn't have a tripod, I couldn't hold the camera still to save my life, and the first attempt was an overall failure. I read up a little bit on tips for moon photography and went back out that night. This time, using my car's roof as a support, I was able to get the best photo of the moon that I had ever taken in my life.

From there, it snowballed. I purchased a used 500mm Opteka telephoto lens for a bargain. Any clear night, I was out taking photos of the moon. Each time getting clearer and clearer shots. My first opportunity to photograph a Falcon 9 launch went incredibly, though certainly room for improvement. I plan on going out as much as possible to continue capturing the launches. Of course, my progress in this hobby will be tracked though my photographs posted here as well as anything I find useful on the Learning page.
